About

There is nothing more precious
than nature.

I became a veterinarian because I wanted to heal, protect, and advocate for animals. I understood early on that saving individual animals was inseparable from saving the systems they depend on. I have learned through my life that we depend on those animals – those systems – just as much as they depend on us. Our future is enmeshed with that of the natural world.

Over the past decade, I've worked across three continents and with dozens of species - from California condors to giant pandas to pangolins - at world-class institutions, including the Smithsonian Conservation Biology Institute, Cornell University, the San Diego Zoo Safari Park, and Fossil Rim Wildlife Center. My training is in zoological medicine, but my practice is fundamentally transdisciplinary: I believe the most durable, holistic conservation outcomes come from braiding veterinary science with ecology, human health, indigenous ways of knowing, earth/climate sciences, sociology, policy, economics, and community engagement.

I'm currently based in Southern California, where I maintain an active practice as a licensed DVM - working across multiple veterinary hospitals and private practices throughout the state. Alongside that, I serve as the Program Manager at Save Pangolins, contribute to the California Condor Recovery Program and local mountain lion research, am the Program Officer for the IUCN Species Survival Commission Wildlife Health Specialist Group, and stay engaged with international One Health initiatives through smaller projects and personally-driven training. I recently graduated from the Emerging Wildlife Conservation Leaders program, which included the successful completion of a collaborative project centered around human-lion conflict management in Africa.

I seek to grow beyond the white coat, having recognized that the greatest impact I can have will be through big-picture, systems-level work. My interests are deliberately broad. I'm as comfortable in the field as I am in policy and diplomatic settings, and am drawn to questions of how entrepreneurship, technology, and creative collaboration can accelerate conservation impact. I am keenly interested in the challenges at the interface of human, animal, and environmental health – issues such as human-wildlife conflict, food security, habitat encroachment, wildlife trafficking, zoonosis/spillover, and the biodiversity crisis.

The problems are large and ever-evolving. Bold, creative solutions are necessary. Through collaboration, community-based conservation, and context-specific approaches, a healthier future is possible. I want to help drive that future.

🇿🇲 Zambia · South Luangwa July '25

A leopard at the riverbank, and a clinic without walls.

Watched this leopard hold the riverbank at golden hour — five impala on the far side, completely unbothered. They knew, somehow, the distance was enough.

Same week, in the bush clinic: a different kind of work. Anesthesia under a tin roof, no fluorescent lights, no familiar machines. You learn fast what's essential and what isn't.

Published Research

Peer-reviewed work
and field contributions.

2021

Validation of a portable point-of-care test for pregnancy diagnosis in non-domestic ruminants

Assessed pregnancy-associated glycoprotein (PAG) detection across four ungulate species at White Oak Conservation Center — gerenuk, eastern giant eland, dama gazelle, and okapi. The test demonstrated 100% sensitivity and specificity in gerenuk and eland, with potential applications for field-based herd health monitoring, genetic management, and species conservation programs.

Journal of Zoo and Wildlife Medicine · Cornell University College of Veterinary Medicine

Ongoing

One Health approaches to pangolin conservation and trafficking interdiction

Applied research in support of Save Pangolins' mission to protect all eight pangolin species. Focuses on the intersection of wildlife health, trade networks, community livelihoods, and international conservation policy.
